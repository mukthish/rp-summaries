**ZeroG (Zero-shot transferability in Graphs)** is a novel framework designed to enable cross-dataset zero-shot transfer learning for graph data. While foundation models like large language models (LLMs) excel at zero-shot learning in NLP, graph models traditionally struggle to generalize to completely new, unseen datasets without dataset-specific and label-specific fine-tuning,.

The paper identifies two main categories of challenges in cross-dataset graph transfer:

1. **Limitations of Traditional Graph Neural Networks (GNNs):** GNNs face "dimension misalignment" because different datasets use varying shallow embeddings (like bag-of-words or TF-IDF) that create incompatible feature sizes,. They also suffer from mismatched label spaces due to fixed classification heads, and they are prone to "negative transfer," where they overfit to the pre-training data's unique structural semantics,.
2. **Limitations of LLMs on Graphs:** While LLMs possess strong reasoning capabilities, applying them directly as classifiers for graphs risks data leakage (having memorized benchmark test data during pre-training) and fails to incorporate the essential structural topology of the graph.

To overcome these barriers, the ZeroG framework integrates LLM text processing with graph structure learning through the following methodologies:

- **Unified Graph Representation:** To solve dimension misalignment, ZeroG uses a pre-trained language model (specifically Sentence-BERT) to independently encode both the textual attributes of the nodes and the natural language descriptions of the target classes,,. This maps all varied dataset features into a standardized, shared semantic space and reformulates node classification from a traditional classification head into a direct text similarity matching task.
- **Prompt-based Subgraph Sampling:** To prevent negative transfer and ensure both semantic and structural information are preserved, ZeroG extracts multi-hop subgraphs from source datasets,. It introduces a novel **semantic-enhanced prompting node** into each subgraph, which acts as a unique identifier carrying a broad textual description of the source dataset's overall context. The framework then applies a neighborhood aggregation process, iteratively updating node features by aggregating them with the features of their immediate neighbors (including the prompting node) to capture structural topology.
- **Lightweight Pre-training (LoRA):** Because full-parameter fine-tuning of an LLM carries a high risk of overfitting in zero-shot settings, ZeroG utilizes Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). By injecting small, trainable low-rank matrices into the transformer layers, the model learns upstream semantics while maintaining its foundational zero-shot capabilities.
- **Downstream Inference:** During inference on a completely unseen target dataset, the pre-trained LLM generates embeddings for the new nodes and classes, captures local structures through neighborhood aggregation, and simply assigns nodes to the class with the highest dot-product similarity score without any further parameter updates,.

**Key Findings and Performance:**

- **In-Domain Dominance:** Tested across citation networks and co-purchase networks, ZeroG significantly outperforms structure-only (Graph SSL) and semantics-only (pure LLM) baselines,. Impressively, on the Pubmed dataset, ZeroG achieved a zero-shot accuracy of 78.02%, matching the performance of traditional semi-supervised methods like GCN and GAT (79.00%) that actually require labeled target data,.
- **Cross-Domain Adaptability:** When transferring knowledge across entirely different domains (e.g., pre-training on Amazon co-purchase networks to classify Wiki-CS web links), ZeroG experienced an expected performance drop but still consistently outperformed state-of-the-art zero-shot baselines like OFA.
- **High Parameter Efficiency:** Thanks to LoRA, ZeroG only requires tuning 0.07 million parameters, drastically less than the 20.7 million required by OFA or the 66.4 million needed for full-parameter tuning. Ablation studies proved that using full-parameter tuning instead of LoRA led to massive overfitting, dropping accuracy by over 50% on the Cora dataset. Furthermore, removing the prompting node or the neighborhood aggregation severely degraded the model's performance, validating the necessity of these structural integrations.
